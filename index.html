Why?

Our mission is simple: to protect and preserve the incredible diversity of animal life on our planet. We believe in living in harmony with nature, rather than standing by as species vanish forever. Together, we can make a difference. By understanding the value of every living creature and taking action, we can ensure a future where animals thrive alongside us. Join us in this vital journey to save and safeguard our precious wildlife



Saving animals is not solely the responsibility of conservationists or experts—it starts with each one of us. By making conscious choices and taking small actions in our daily lives, we can create a significant impact. Whether it's reducing our carbon footprint, supporting sustainable practices, or advocating for stronger conservation policies, we have the power to protect and preserve the incredible creatures we share this planet with. Together, let's embrace our role as guardians of nature and leave a legacy of compassion and coexistence for generations to come

Do's and Dont's

To truly conserve and protect animals, it's crucial not only to focus on endangered species but also to pay attention to the everyday animals we encounter. By ending the harmful habits that adversely affect these animals, we can prevent their decline and extinction. It's up to us to take the initiative and make a difference in saving these animals



Do's

  • Adopting sustainable and eco-friendly practices in daily life, such as reducing plastic use, conserving water, and promoting renewable energy sources.
  • Spreading awareness about endangered species and conservation efforts through social media, educational campaigns, or community events.
  • Participating in citizen science projects or wildlife monitoring programs to contribute to scientific research and data collection.
  • Encouraging responsible wildlife tourism by choosing ethical operators and respecting animal habitats and behavior.
  • Supporting legislation and policies that protect endangered species and their habitats.


Dont's

  • Avoiding the purchase of products made from endangered species or derived from illegal wildlife trade.
  • Refraining from supporting activities that exploit animals for entertainment purposes, such as circuses with animal performances or petting wild animals without proper permits.
  • Not engaging in activities that disturb or harass wildlife, such as feeding or approaching animals too closely.
  • Avoiding the release of non-native species into the wild, which can disrupt ecosystems and harm native species.
  • Refraining from buying or keeping exotic pets without proper knowledge and understanding of their care requirements.

Endangered Species

overview of some endangered species in the world which need help to thrive in this world







Orangutan

Known for their distinctive red fur, orangutans are the largest arboreal mammal, spending most of their time in trees. Long, powerful arms and grasping hands and feet allow them to move through the branches. These great apes share 96.4% of our genes and are highly intelligent creatures.

Chimpanzee

Like us, chimps are highly social animals, care for their offspring for years and can live to be over 50. In fact, chimpanzees are our closest cousins; we share about 98% of our genes.

Tiger

Tigers are carnivorous mammals that prey on organisms like deer. They are quick-paced and are very fast. The body of tigers are in the color of red or orange with dark black stripes on the surface. They have a length of 6 to 10 feet and weight nearly 660 pounds when they reach maturity. Tigers have a ferocious roar, but they don’t interfere with humans unless they hit or cause them harm

Asian Elephant

The Asian elephant is the largest land mammal on the Asian continent. They inhabit dry to wet forest and grassland habitats in 13 range countries spanning South and Southeast Asia. While they have preferred forage plants, Asian elephants have adapted to surviving on resources that vary based on the area.

Ethiopian Wolf

Native to Ethiopia, these long-limbed, slender canids are some of the most endangered animals in Africa. They have a black, bushy tail that can reach up to 40 centimeters in length, pointed ears, and a slender snout. They are tawny red with a white underbelly and blaze on their chests, and also have white fur on their throats, which sweeps up and covers the underside of their muzzle. The female wolves tend to be paler in color than males and are smaller overall.

Snow Leopard

Snow leopards have evolved to live in some of the harshest conditions on Earth. Their thick white-gray coat spotted with large black rosettes blends in perfectly with Asia’s steep and rocky, high mountains. Because of their incredible natural camouflage, rendering them almost invisible in their surroundings, snow leopards are often referred to as the “ghost of the mountains.”

Black Rhinoceros

The black rhinoceros are the smaller of the two African rhino species. Black rhinos have hooked upper lips and they are browsers, rather than grazers. This means that they are herbivores who do not feed on low-growing vegetation, and their pointed lip helps them feed on leaves from bushes and trees. They have two horns, which grow continuously from the skin at their base throughout their life.

Leatherback Turtle

Leatherback turtles are named for their shell which is leather-like rather than hard, like other turtles. They are the largest sea turtle species and also one of the most migratory, crossing both the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. Pacific leatherbacks migrate from nesting beaches in the Coral Triangle all the way to the California coast to feed on the abundant jellyfish every summer and fall.

Mountain Gorilla

As their name implies, mountain gorillas live in forests high in the mountains, at elevations of 8,000 to 13,000 feet. They have thicker fur, and more of it, compared to other great apes. The fur helps them to survive in a habitat where temperatures often drop below freezing. But as humans have moved more and more into the gorillas’ territory, the gorillas have been pushed farther up into the mountains for longer periods, forcing them to endure dangerous and sometimes deadly conditions.

Javan Rhinoceros

This species is a dusky grey color and has a single horn of up to about 10 inches. Its skin has a number of loose folds, giving the appearance of armor plating. The Javan rhino is very similar in appearance to the closely-related greater one-horned rhinoceros, but has a much smaller head and less apparent skin folds.
